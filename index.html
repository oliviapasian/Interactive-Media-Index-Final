Reading Response 1

Reading 1: How to Crit

How to Crit by Mitch Goldstein

1. What are some challenges you face when giving a critique?

I think one thing that challenges me with critique is feeling like I'm being harsh. It never is my intention to be and I don't think I am, but it can feel uncomfortable for me to talk about things that might not be working with someones work. This is because it sometimes can be hard for me to receive "negative" feedback so it can be hard to give it out knowing how I sometimes feel about it. But in the reading, on the main page, the author wrote that "critique serves the work, not the person who made the work", which does change my perspective on it. I of course always stick to the work itself anyway, but reading that and other parts such as "you should walk away from getting a crit feeling empowered and excited to make the work better" does make me feel more comfortable as it reminds me that the purpose is to learn and to improve our work, so as long as feedback is based on that it will be a positive experience.

2. How do you handle a critique that you disagree with?

As I mentioned, something I struggle with is focusing on the educational part of critique and not taking it personally, so I am working on being better about being more receptive to critiques I don't necessarily like or agree with. If I don't agree with something, I still usually consider it anyway before immediately disregarding it because there still might be something to consider with the feedback as they gave it for a reason. This section in the "getting feedback" page was helpful for me with considering this idea: "Everything is up for interpretation — but also note that ignoring what everybody says probably won’t help your work improve." I want to be able to think about ALL the feedback I receive and see what I can use from it.

3. Why do you think it’s important to give and receive critiques?

I think critique is important because it is an opportunity to get other perspectives on your work and see how others might interpret it. I sometimes get feedback about how someone saw my work differently than I intended which gives me insight as to whether I should run with that or change it to match my intentions better. And, since I created the work I usually have an attachment to it, so hearing from others about issues I was too close to see is helpful. I thought this section in the reading was interesting and not something I really considered: "Design school is about being active, not being passive. If you need more, it is up to you to find it." Critique encourages us to use design language and communicate with others about our work, as well as be able to know what questions to ask to get helpful feedback. I think it important to be able to talk about your work and the work of others to better understand design terminology and know what areas need improvement.

What Screens Want by Frank Chimero

1. Where do you stand with the two ideological camps: flat and skeuo? For example, do you think one is "better" than the other or do you strongly believe in one or the other.

I don’t have a strong feeling about one or the other. I think it really is just about personal preference rather than one being inherently “better” than the other. I think I’m a bit more on the skeuo side because I think it makes things more clear. By that I mean it adds clarity by adding more detail and texture and 3 dimensionality, therefore it looks more real and is easier to identify. With flat, it can be a bit harder to tell exactly what it is sometimes and feels harder on the eyes to me. And as mentioned in the reading, I don’t think it necessarily matters, because a computer will be able to make both visible and there are usually ways to cater to individual preference.

The zoopraxiscope is something I hadn’t heard of before this reading. It is basically a glass disc with images on it that when spun at high speeds with light behind it creates a moving picture projection. As the reading mentions, it’s similar to design, particularly web and interaction design, because of the focus on movement, the use of screens, and creating interactions using time and change. I don’t think I agree, I think that screens and digital design has a lot of variety in possibilities. Reading Response 3

Reading 3: Touring Complete User

Touring Complete User by Olia Lialina

1. What do you think is Lialina's opinion of the word "user"?

I think Lialina thinks the word “user” is a bit of a double-edged sword, as she describes how there can be an assumption with web developers that users are perhaps more “stupid” (Lialina’s word choice, not mine: “...front end developers, don’t assume that your users are more stupid than you, they are just busy”) for not necessarily being able to write their own code, but there is also a sort of marketing towards users that programming is not fun or not creative (as described in Lialaina’s paragraph about the Adobe marketing: “...the less you think about source code, scripts, links and the web itself, the more creative you are as a web designer”). Lialina also describes the “alienation of users from their computers” which I think ties in with what she think of the word “user”, because I think she sees the word as a way to alienate those who are perhaps less engaged with their computer usage. Lialina also said the following: “I would say it is wrong to say either you understand computers or u are the user”. I think that she feels “users” should not be as alienated from their computers as they are by the usage of the word “user”, and I think she wants there to be more of a spectrum when it comes to how we use computers as opposed to categorising by either someone who understands computers or is a user.

2. What is Lialina's ideal internet user? Provide some of the qualities that she outlines.

I think Universal users (which she describes as “users who have the ability to achieve their goals regardless of the primary purpose of an application or device”) are Lialina’s ideal internet user. She says that a “Universal User’s mind set (it is a mind set, not set of rules, not a vow) means to liaise with hardware and software” and that in terms of Interface Design and UX, this kind of interaction “is the ultimate experience”. She seems to praise the idea of cooperating with technology rather than just using it. Lialina also says that Universal Users “will find a way to their aspiration without an app or utility programmed specifically for it. The Universal user is not a super user, not half a hacker”. Universal Users are many of us to some degree and Lialina is saying that this kind of user is very common and that they are not either a hacker or a regular user, they are their own category of someone who visits websites and makes the experience their own. The idea of escaping the “user” label as a negative term comes up a lot in Lialina’s essay, and so I think her ideal user is someone who is technically a user but escapes the negative connotations of the word by not being “very busy” and instead challenging what computers can do.

3. From your own experiences interacting on the web—what is an example where there are "gaps that can be filled by users"?

Lialina defined the "gaps that can be filled by users" as “where smoothness and seamlessness are broken and some of the final links in the chain are left for the users to complete”. I think interactive experiences where users can input text or press buttons to customise their experience are the gaps that can be filled by users. An example coming to mind, that I think technically wouldn’t necessarily be a website, would be the Sims games. You can use sliders and colour pickers and buttons to customise a character, name it, and then use controls to design a life for them. I’m sure there is a much better example out there, but I think that kind of interaction where it is very open but also has limitations with what kinds of changes you can make is the kind of gap that users can fill.

Reading 4: My website is a shifting house next to a river of knowledge

My website is a shifting house next to a river of knowledge by Laurel Schwulst

1. Why does Laurel Schwulst advocate for individual websites over social media? Do you agree with her? Please explain your answer.

I think Schwulst advocates for individual websites over social media because she believes that the web is about individuality and creativity rather than about “being powered by a small handful of big corporations—like Facebook/Instagram, Twitter, and Google”. I agree with her, I think the web was created for people to connect with each other and leave their own mark, and individual websites are a really neat form of expression and are a lot more lively than social media. Individual websites create a much more personal experience with the web and they are how we make the web “actually work for people” instead of corporations. I also think that what Schwulst says about steering the web “back to its original architecture simply by having a website“ and how “artists, in particular, could be instrumental in this space—showing the world where the web can go” is further reasoning from her about supporting individual websites. I think that this point about artists having an instrumental role in bringing back the original ideas behind the web is interesting, and I agree because I think the creative and unique web designs from artists are much closer to the original ideas for the web over the massive social media websites that are much less experimental and individual.

Reading 5: the Poetry of Tools

The Poetry of Tools by Mindy Seu

1. In the opening paragraph of her post, Seu asks you, the reader to join her to 'defamiliarize our tools.' What do you think she means by that? And how do you think that action relates to her use of the word poetry?

I think the meaning of 'defamiliarizing our tools' is that we are familiar with things like clicking and scrolling to the point where we don't even think about those interactions and we are missing out on opportunity to reconsider those tools as design elements in their own right. Seu poses this question: "How can we reconsider the actions we perform in our daily interfaces?" And then adds that "identifying the constraints of these tools can help us recognize their poetic potential". I think she is saying that while basic web actions can seem mundane, they can be repurposed to create interesting interactions on the web. Poetry is about turning words into an expression of ideas and emotions, so I think the usage of the word poetry is about turning simple web actions into something meaningful.

2. Seu outlines 6 categories within her taxonomy of tool poetics (Slow Growth & Slow Death, Early Works, Performing Data, Removing Function from Form, Production of Errors, & Regeneration). Choose at least one of these categories and define it in your own words. Now find a website or online interface* not mentioned in her post that you feel operates within the chosen definition. Explain why.

I think Removing Function from Form is about stripping down web tools to the bare bones and creating designs using only basic interactions and forms. The examples shown are things like shaping a bunch of checkboxes into different designs and composing new designs out of common forms. I think this category is for websites that take simple forms that we see every day and turn them into something different that often is different from the intended purpose. The example I found is this website here. It makes use of very basic symbols and lays them out into the shapes of houses. The houses are all clickable links and none of it has been styled, like the links turn purple after being clicked and the colours are the original blue of links and a white background. I think it fits in the Removing Function from Form category because it uses very simple forms and minimal styling to create an interesting and unique composition that is not what the symbols and letterforms used were meant for.
